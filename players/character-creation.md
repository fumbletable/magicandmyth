# Creating a Character

Character creation is a three-step process. It begins with a concept. Take time to envision the type of character you wish to play, whether it be a trickster rogue, battle-hardened warrior or master of arcane magic. Picture his or her look and personality. Strengths? Weaknesses? Have fun with this. The rules can wait.

The next step is talking to your Game Master. Your GM should understand what you're driving at primarily to determine whether the concept clashes with the planned campaign, but also to determine whether the rules work for you as written. The rules for character creation were not written to accommodate every single possible character concept. Fear not. The GM has discretion to bend the rules to accommodate your concept, provided it's not irresponsible.

Suppose you want to play an illusionist wizard that grew up in a traveling circus. Although your character is a wizard by trade, his years on the road, performing for crowds, has shaped the way he casts magic. You want your character to weave oration and impersonation into his illusion magic. In this situation, your GM might allow you to take the Performance nonweapon proficiency as if you had in-class access to it and you might be allowed to lace your illusions with dramatic flair.

At this point, you sit down with dice, a pen and some paper. Work through the next several chapters to create your Magic&Myth character. The following section takes you through the process step-by-step.

## Step-By-Step Walkthrough

**Attributes (Chapter 1):** Unless your GM is rolling attributes for the players, grab some dice and paper, and head over to [Attributes](attributes). There are six possible methods of generating attributes with dice: Default, Legacy, Point Buy, Series, Loaded Dice, and Heroic. Check with your GM to see which he prefers. With an eye toward your concept, assign a score to each of your attributes.

**Ancestries (Chapter 2):** Once you assign scores to your attributes, you choose an ancestry from the following: [dwarf](ancestries/dwarf), [elf](ancestries/elf), [gnome](ancestries/gnome), [half-elf](ancestries/half-elf), [half-orc](ancestries/half-orc), [halfling](ancestries/halfling), and [human](ancestries/human). All ancestries modify your attributes. Most increase one strong attribute and decrease another, while humans allow you to add a +1 to any attribute. Also bear in mind that some class/ancestry combinations work better than others, and some ancestries can't enter a certain class without GM permission.

**Classes (Chapter 3):** There are ten core classes: [barbarian](classes/barbarian), [bard](classes/bard), [cleric](classes/cleric), [druid](classes/druid), [fighter](classes/fighter), [monk](classes/monk), [paladin](classes/paladin), [ranger](classes/ranger), [thief](classes/thief) and [wizard](classes/wizard). Each lends to an entirely different play experience. Fighters, paladins and rangers are the warrior-types. Clerics and druids are divine spellcasters charged with the advancement of their religious beliefs. Bards and thieves are skill-laden rogues with unmatched social skills. Wizards are arcane spellcasters. Choose one (or a combination of two if multi-classing) and record the information for Level 1 on your class's level chart on your character sheet.

**Weapon Proficiencies (Chapter 4):** Each class starts at Level 1 with a certain amount of weapon proficiency slots. You spend these slots in the [Weapon Proficiencies](proficiencies/weapon-proficiencies) chapter. The most commonly used proficiency is weapon use, which allows you to use a weapon with no penalty. Your class may also allow you to choose specialization, or weapon group, as well. There are a few weapon proficiencies that increase your character's chances at defense and called shots, as well.

**Nonweapon Proficiencies (Chapter 5):** Each class starts with a number of nonweapon proficiency slots. You spend these slots on [nonweapon proficiencies](proficiencies/nonweapon-proficiencies), which are non-combat skills, such as rope-use, acrobatics, spellcraft and knowledge of the dark arts. Check your class list to determine to which lists of nonweapon proficiencies you have access.

**Class Talents (Chapter 6):** If your GM is allowing class talents, your 1st-level character will have one to choose from among the list provided for your class in the Class Talents chapter. Class talents are chosen to differentiate members of the same class. They provide a boost to either the mechanical or thematic aspects of the class. All classes receive one class talent at 1st level and another talent every 3rd level thereafter.

**Equipment (Chapter 7):** Your character starts with a certain amount of gold pieces. Each class starts with a range of starting gold; the actual amount is randomly determined by dice. The [Equipment](equipment/) chapter includes hundreds of different weapons, suits of armor, adventuring gear and general items. You certainly want weapons and armor to start, but some of the less obvious items, such as chalk, powder and mirrors, come in handy too.

**The Character Sheet:** After you jot down your character's attributes, ancestry, class, proficiencies and class talents, transfer it all to the standard Character Sheet. This record of statistics couples with your created character history to form your Player Character. You're now ready to play.

## MAGIC System

With the exception of attacks and a few corner cases, such as parries and opposed attribute checks, your rolls will be gauged against the MAGIC System. Every task you wish to perform will have a designated difficulty from Minor to Colossal. The difficulties have a required minimum result that must be achieved on a d20 roll. (The various d20 modifiers are discussed in the upcoming chapters.)

### Difficulty Chart

| Difficulty | TN | Grittier Option |
|-----------|-----|----------------|
| Minor | 5 | 10 |
| Average | 10 | 15 |
| Great | 15 | 20 |
| Incredible | 20 | 25 |
| Colossal | 25 | 30 |

**Successful Checks:** To succeed on a task, your modified roll must equal or exceed the required result for the Difficulty called for. If your GM asks you to succeed on an Incredible check, for example, your modified roll must equal or exceed 20; if the rules require an Average result, your modified roll must equal or exceed 10.

**Shorthand:** Difficulties and their required checks appear throughout the game in this fashion: Minor (TN 5). In this example, the difficulty is Minor and the required result is 5 or more.

**Grittier Option:** For gaming groups preferring a grittier and more difficult scale, use the grittier option presented here. The game manual assumes the use of the standard "TN" column, so remember to replace the TN references in the rules, if the grittier option makes sense for you.

### Difficulties

The adjectives (Minor, Average, etc.) help the GM and players conceptualize the different levels of difficulty. Thus, by putting the following to memory, you and your GM can quickly master the MAGIC System:

**Minor:** Minor tasks are very simple. In most cases, the average person will succeed on a minor task 75% of the time. Climbing up a 10 foot wall with many places to grip and stand is Minor. All checks that equal or exceed 5 succeed on Minor tasks.

**Average:** Average tasks require a bit more concentration and skill. The average person succeeds on an Average task about half the time. Bashing down a standard wooden door is an Average task. All checks that equal or exceed 10 succeed on Average tasks.

**Great:** Great tasks require a great set of skills or a great exercise of one's skill. The average person only succeeds on Great tasks 25% of the time. Lifting up a standard portcullis in the sewers is a Great task. All checks that equal or exceed 15 succeed on Great tasks.

**Incredible:** These are the truly awesome tasks that average people succeed with luck alone. The average person has only a 5% chance of succeeding here. Breaking through a metal jail cell door is an Incredible task. All checks that equal or exceed 20 succeed on Incredible tasks.

**Colossal:** These are impossible for an average person to perform. Colossal tasks are only possible for the very skilled or the very gifted. Using the alley wall of two adjacent buildings to scale up 40 feet in one movement is Colossal. Bending the thick bars of a new jail cell to escape is Colossal. All checks that equal or exceed 25 succeed on Colossal tasks.

### The Game In Play

In some situations, the TN (i.e. Target Number) is set by the game rules or adventure module. The GM requests whatever Difficulty is listed. In other situations, the GM has full discretion to gauge the difficulty of a task. If you want to jump across a wide cavern, bluff a priest, recall information you may have learned, the GM looks at the Difficulty Chart and chooses a TN that fits the circumstance. He or she has full discretion in this regard.

As a player, the Difficulty Chart works to your advantage as well. Suppose whatever you wish to perform requires an Average check. You roll into the Incredible bracket by meeting or beating TN 20. You have over-performed and the results are relative. Perhaps you jumped across the chasm with ease, landing softly on your feet, prepared for battle. Perhaps you gained the trust of a priest from your opposing church. Perhaps you not only remembered the information you were hoping to recall, but also gleaned a valuable secret as well.

The above examples are reactive uses of the MAGIC System. The GM required a certain TN and the player rolled high enough to pull off something extraordinary. As a player, you can be more proactive. Use the MAGIC System to perform amazing feats. If you're faced with a door behind which your enemies lie, you're not limited to simply breaking it down. You can leap through the door, landing prepped for battle and grabbing surprise. The GM rates your action on the Difficulty Chart and asks for a TN.

Magic&Myth is easy to use. Over time, you will find it to be a great source of imaginative gaming.
